The Ministry of Health (MOH) already offers free screening through the Sehhaty app (24M users). The tools exist — people just aren't using them. This project closes that gap with behavioral science and targeted messaging. This platform complements the existing Taakkad (تأكد) national screening initiative — Taakkad built the screening clinics; we fill them with behavioral demand. 57.8% of Saudi diabetics are undiagnosed (El Bcheraoui et al. 2014, SHIS national survey, n=10,735), representing millions who need to be reached.
Sources: IDF Diabetes Atlas 2024, Frontiers in Clinical Diabetes 2024
Evidence base: Simple text reminders alone increase screening uptake by 5–17 percentage points (CDC Community Guide, CareMessage RCT at $4.01/person, NHS BIT at £3.70/person). Our goal: can behavioral science do even better?
Solution

Diagnose → Intervene → Learn

An adaptive messaging platform that identifies each user's behavioral barrier, delivers a matched intervention, and optimizes through continuous learning.

Diagnose

SADRISC (Saudi Diabetes Risk Score) + COM-B (Capability, Opportunity, Motivation – Behaviour) barrier diagnosis + TTM (Transtheoretical Model) stage classification — all via WhatsApp. HBM's 'cues to action' construct explains why messaging triggers behavior — our platform IS the cue.

Intervene

The system selects a behavioral framework based on the user's barrier, then Claude (Anthropic's AI) generates Arabic messages, which pass through a 5-layer validation pipeline. Foundation layer: opt-out default effect (Mehta et al. 2018: opt-out screening 29.1% vs opt-in 9.6%). We apply the same principle — pre-reserve appointments, let messaging prevent cancellation rather than generate booking.

Learn

Thompson Sampling with factored action space optimizes across 5 message design dimensions independently. Each message is parameterized by framing, social proof, reflection, content, and urgency — Thompson Sampling learns per-dimension posteriors with HeartSteps-style dosage control (lambda=0.95) for fatigue prevention.

Target behavior: Get the user to book a free diabetes screening through the Sehhaty national health app. Every design decision serves this single goal.

Behavioral Science

Each user stage triggers a different evidence-based intervention

The Transtheoretical Model provides a practical segmentation framework — classifying users by readiness signals to route appropriate messages. The causal model of behavior change is COM-B, which diagnoses the specific barrier preventing action. We use COM-B as the diagnostic layer, then operationalize through HBM cues-to-action — addressing the known limitation that COM-B alone does not specify intervention mechanisms (Thinking About Behavior, 2024).

TTM — Prochaska & DiClemente, 1983
How COM-B works in practice: When a user replies, WhatsApp opens a 24-hour session. We ask "What prevents you from getting screened?" via an interactive list. Their answer maps to a COM-B barrier (e.g. "I don't have time" = Opportunity barrier), and the system selects a matching intervention. Barrier-matched interventions are ~56% more effective (Baker 2015 Cochrane review, OR=1.56, 95% CI 1.27–1.93). Saudi-specific: Health decisions are family-mediated — Social Opportunity means family permission. Messages target the family unit ("Take your father for screening").

Message Library Pattern: AI generates 15 candidates → behavioral scientists curate → system selects from pre-approved library at send time. NOT real-time generation.
Arabic NLP: Claude handles Arabic natively. OSMAN formula for readability (simple math, no ML). Regex for PII detection. Keyword matching for WhatsApp response classification. AraBERT/MARBERT deferred to Phase 2 when response volume justifies complexity.

Composite Priority Score

composite = 0.4 × (risk/15) + 0.25 × (1 - HES/100) + 0.35 × propensity

High risk + low engagement + high propensity = highest priority

HES — Health Engagement Score

HES = Σ(weight_i × metric_i × e^(-λt)) λ = ln(2)/14 // 14-day half-life Components: login freq (25%) feature depth (25%) content consumption (25%) health actions (25%)

Control arm: "Your free screening is reserved for you" — Milkman's ownership frame (+5–6.7% across 680K). If personalization can't beat this, we save complexity.
Headline metric: NNT (Number Needed to Treat) — how many messages to produce one additional screening? Lower is better.
Approach validated by GAMBITTS (NeurIPS 2025) — Thompson Sampling + LLM-generated messages for mobile health interventions. Also: REINFORCE trial (npj Digital Medicine 2024, +13.6% adherence improvement). Known limitation: Zhang 2025 shows Thompson Sampling variance is underestimated 5–10x — we address this with smoothed selection (softmax instead of argmax) and replicability-aware evaluation design.

Closing

Honest about limitations — and clear on what we'd build next

Honest Trade-offs

COM-B diagnosis relies on self-reported barriers — users may not know what stops them. Plan: validate against behavioral signals in Phase 1.

Loss-framing advantage for diabetes screening is NOT proven (Griffin 2010: NS). Use as A/B variant, never as universal default.

MOH/NPHIES integration requires regulatory approval — timeline risk. Phase 0 uses self-reported data; Phase 1 validates against Sehhaty appointment API.

WhatsApp template approval: 47% first-submission rejection rate. Mitigation: submit 5-8 core shells 4-6 weeks before pilot.

With More Time

Interactive demo built — available at github.com/Hus-Mek/behaviorsync-demo. All 4 screens shown in this deck.
